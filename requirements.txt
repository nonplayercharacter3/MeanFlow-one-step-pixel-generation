LR-1: MeanFlow — pixel-space one-step generative model (GPU / PyTorch)
Track: Latent Reasoning. This track has two tasks — LR-1 (MeanFlow) and LR-2 (Coconut). Pick exactly one.

Overview
Reproduce MeanFlow for one-step image generation in pixel space, implemented in PyTorch on GPU. The official reference implementation is written for TPU (JAX / Tunix); your job is to port the idea to a clean GPU/PyTorch training loop and demonstrate that it learns.

We are not asking for a SOTA result. We want to see that you understand the MeanFlow objective, can implement the average-velocity / mean-flow identity correctly, and can get a model to actually train and generate.

For broader context on why latent-space search is interesting for reasoning, read the LaDiR paper and the LaDi-RL paper. Use them as conceptual background; this task does not ask you to reproduce either paper.

AI usage: Fully allowed (coding agents, copilots, etc.). You must understand everything you submit.

What MeanFlow is (in one paragraph)
Flow matching learns an instantaneous velocity v(z_t, t) and generates by integrating an ODE over many steps. MeanFlow instead learns the average velocity u(z_t, r, t) between two times r < t, defined as u(z_t, r, t) = (1/(t-r)) ∫_r^t v(z_τ, τ) dτ. This satisfies the MeanFlow identity relating the average velocity to the instantaneous velocity via its total derivative (the term computed with a JVP / jvp). Training on this identity lets you sample in a single step: x_0 ≈ x_1 - u(x_1, 0, 1) (one network evaluation), instead of an N-step ODE solve.

Hints
Start with the overfit-3 test before anything else. It is the fastest signal that your loss, your (r, t) sampling, and your one-step sampler agree. If 3 images won't overfit, scaling up won't help.
The mean-flow identity needs a directional derivative of u w.r.t. (z_t, t) along the flow. In PyTorch use torch.func.jvp (functorch) — this is the single trickiest part of the port from JAX's jax.jvp. Get the tangent vector right: it pairs (v, 0..., 1) for inputs (z_t, r, t).
Use a stop-gradient on the JVP target term (matching the paper) — gradients flow only through the u prediction, not through the target.
Sample r and t with r ≤ t; include a fraction of r == t (reduces to plain flow matching) to stabilize early training. Document your sampling distribution.
Keep the backbone tiny and the batch fixed while debugging. Log the loss every step.
Pixel space at low resolution avoids needing a VAE — keep it simple.
